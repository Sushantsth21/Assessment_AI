
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) - Influenza (Flu)

Overview:
Influenza (also known as “flu”) is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It can cause mild to severe illness and at times can lead to death. Influenza viruses infect the nose, throat, and lungs.

Signs and Symptoms:
- Sudden onset of fever or chills
- Cough
- Sore throat
- Runny or stuffy nose
- Muscle or body aches
- Headaches
- Fatigue (tiredness)
- Vomiting and diarrhea (more common in children)

Flu Complications:
- Pneumonia
- Sinus and ear infections
- Inflammation of the heart, brain, or muscle tissues
- Multi-organ failure
- Worsening of chronic conditions (e.g., asthma, heart disease)

People at Higher Risk:
- Adults over 65
- Children under 5, especially under 2
- Pregnant women
- Individuals with chronic conditions (asthma, diabetes, heart disease)

Emergency Warning Signs:
Children:
- Fast breathing or trouble breathing
- Bluish lips or face
- Chest pain
- Dehydration or not urinating
- High or uncontrolled fever
Adults:
- Difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Persistent dizziness or confusion
- Seizures
- Severe muscle pain or weakness

Prevention:
- Get a flu vaccine every year (recommended for everyone 6 months and older)
- Avoid close contact with sick people
- Stay home when sick
- Wash hands frequently
- Cover coughs/sneezes and disinfect surfaces
- Improve air quality in indoor spaces

Treatment:
- Antiviral drugs (prescription only) can shorten illness duration and prevent complications
- Most effective when started within 48 hours of symptom onset
- Antivirals include oseltamivir (Tamiflu), zanamivir (Relenza), etc.
- Antibiotics are not effective against flu

Comparison of Cold vs Flu:
- Flu has more abrupt onset and is more likely to include high fever, aches, and severe fatigue
